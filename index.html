BSIS Capstone · LIS Program
Capstone Reflection Essay
+

This capstone reflection examines how four advanced BSIS courses shaped my professional identity and career direction — and highlights two assignments that fundamentally changed how I think about cybersecurity as a field.

Course Relevance to Career Goals

Four courses stand out as most formative: LIS 3261 Introduction to Information Science, LIS 4204 Information Behaviors, LIS 4482 Networks and Communication, and CIS 4365 Computer Security Policies. Together they gave me a unified view of information science as a convergence of technical systems, human behavior, and organizational strategy.

LIS 4204 introduced me to frameworks like Fisher et al.'s "information grounds" and Belkin's Anomalous State of Knowledge theory — which reframed for me why user-centered thinking matters in cybersecurity. Security policies only work when the people subject to them can understand and follow them. CIS 4365 gave me direct exposure to how policies are built, implemented, and evaluated in real organizations.

Two Impactful Assignments

The penetration testing research paper (LIS 3261) required me to investigate the ethical dimensions of ethical hacking — exploring frameworks like the Penetration Testing Execution Standard (PTES) and the OWASP Testing Guide. It made clear that cybersecurity is a profession with substantial legal and ethical obligations, not just a technical skill set.

The AI-generated cybersecurity policy critique (CIS 4365) challenged me to use an AI tool to produce a policy document, then critically assess it against course standards. The AI output looked polished but lacked risk-based implementation strategies, a formal policy hierarchy, physical security measures, and performance measurement frameworks — which taught me that professional judgment cannot be outsourced to technology.

Conclusion

I entered the BSIS program focused on cybersecurity. I'm leaving with a broader understanding of information science as a field where technology, organizational culture, ethics, and human behavior intersect — and carrying those questions with me into a career devoted to protecting information and the people who depend on it.

Information Policy · Technology in the News
The CREATE AI Act of 2025 & the NAIRR
+

This essay examines the CREATE AI Act of 2025 — bipartisan legislation introduced by Representatives Obernolte and Beyer to formally establish the National Artificial Intelligence Research Resource (NAIRR) as shared national infrastructure for AI research.

The Core Problem

A small number of large technology corporations currently hold the computational infrastructure, proprietary datasets, and processing power required to conduct cutting-edge AI research — effectively excluding universities, nonprofits, and individual researchers. The Stanford AI Index Report 2025 found 51% of organizations cited knowledge and training gaps as their biggest barrier to responsible AI, while 45% cited resource constraints.

What the Bill Proposes

The CREATE AI Act would require the NSF to establish the NAIRR within a year of passage, providing cloud computing, curated datasets, AI testbeds, pre-trained models, and educational resources to researchers, educators, and students connected to U.S. higher education institutions, nonprofits, and federal agencies. Crucially, it mandates a free access tier and requires a significant portion of annual computational resources to be dedicated to AI privacy, ethics, safety, and reliability projects.

Legislative History & Stakeholders

Originally introduced in the 118th Congress (2023) with bipartisan support, the bill was reintroduced in March 2025 with a new clause permitting donated resources from the private sector — raising concerns about corporate influence over a nominally public resource. As of early 2026, it remains pending in committee. The NAIRR pilot had already supported over 600 research projects and engaged more than 6,000 students across all 50 states.

LIS Perspective

From an information science standpoint, the NAIRR represents a chance to align national AI policy with core library values: intellectual freedom, privacy, equity of access, and the democratization of knowledge. Information professionals are uniquely positioned to advocate for this infrastructure, support users in navigating it, and ensure local AI deployments remain governed by ethical standards.

Event Report · Emerging Tech Topics
Cybersecurity & Data Science Lecture Series
+

This report reviews two virtual lectures examining how rapid technological advancement, growing cybersecurity threats, and increased reliance on data are redefining IT careers and organizational structures.

Key Themes

Data and security are inseparable. Both sessions emphasized that data is only valuable when it is secure, accurate, and well-managed — reinforcing why cybersecurity experts and data analysts must collaborate to ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of organizational information.

Cybersecurity is now organizational strategy, not a siloed function. The presenters argued that as cloud-based platforms and networked systems expand, security becomes a core business function requiring professionals who can recognize vulnerabilities, assess risk, and communicate those risks to non-technical leadership.

Human factors drive most incidents. Rather than focusing only on technical vulnerabilities, both sessions highlighted that inadequate user practices, poor training, and lax policies are frequent root causes of breaches — expanding the role of security professionals beyond purely technical problem-solving into communication, user education, and ethical responsibility.

Career Development Takeaways

The lectures emphasized that early-career technology professionals need strong analytical thinking, ethical judgment, and the ability to translate complex technical findings into actionable insights for non-technical stakeholders. Adaptability and commitment to lifelong learning were presented as non-negotiable for long-term success in a field where tools, platforms, and threats evolve continuously.

Sources: Irani, A. (2024). Cybersecurity in the Age of AI — TEDxDESC Youth. | Mishra, A. (2020). Demystifying Data Science — TEDxTalks.

Project Management · IT Planning
University Software-Tracking Database Plan
+

This project management plan outlines the design and development of a centralized software-tracking database for a university IT department — enabling the institution to monitor software titles, vendors, versions, licensing agreements, department usage, and installation locations across campus systems.

The plan follows the six-phase model from the DANS Project Management Handbook: Initiation, Definition, Design, Development, Implementation, and Follow-up.

Phase Summary

  • Initiation: CIO identifies need after internal audit reveals inconsistent software records; project proposal drafted covering scope, stakeholders, timeline, and budget.
  • Definition: Requirements gathered from IT staff, department admins, procurement, and compliance teams. Functional, operational, and governance requirements locked to prevent scope creep.
  • Design: Database architect develops entity-relationship diagrams, data flow models, and user access architectures. Final design selected for maintainability, scalability, and IT standards compliance.
  • Development: Implementation preparations finalized — development rules established, test environments configured, QA protocols created, cybersecurity review completed.
  • Implementation: Database built, populated, and tested. Pilot rollout conducted with College of Arts and Sciences and College of Engineering. System validated against original specifications.
  • Follow-up: Training materials completed, database transferred to IT Asset Management Office, lessons learned documented, project formally closed.

Relevance

This plan demonstrates practical application of IT project governance in a higher education context — where departmental collaboration, licensing compliance, and cybersecurity standards must all be balanced within defined resource and timeline constraints.
